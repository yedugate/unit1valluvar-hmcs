MOLD (MOULD) — Student-Friendly Notes

History • Types • Growth • Temperature • Moisture • Time • Growth Curve • Control • Food Roles

1) What is Mold?

Molds are **multicellular fungi** that grow as long threads called hyphae. A mass of hyphae is a mycelium, seen as fuzzy growth on bread, fruits, grains, etc. They make airborne spores for spread.

FeatureSimple meaningExam hook
Cell typeEukaryote (true nucleus)Fungal cell wall (chitin)
Body planHyphae → myceliumFuzzy/velvety colonies
ReproductionAsexual spores (conidia/sporangia)Colorful spore heads
Oxygen needAerobic (need air)Surface growth common

2) History (very short)

Robert Hooke (1665) illustrated microscopic molds; P.A. Micheli (1729) described fungal spores; A. Fleming (1928) observed Penicillium killing bacteria → **penicillin**.
YearMilestoneWhy it matters
1665Hooke’s drawings of moldsEarly microscopy of fungi
1729Micheli describes sporesExplains mold reproduction
1928Fleming & PenicilliumAntibiotic discovery

7) Molds in Foods — Useful vs Harmful

Beneficial (useful)

GroupWhat they doFoods
Penicillium roqueforti, P. camembertiRipening enzymes → texture/flavorBlue/soft cheeses
Aspergillus oryzaeKoji enzymes (amylases, proteases)Soy sauce, miso

Harmful (spoilage & toxins)

TypeExamplesRisk
Surface spoilageRhizopus, MucorSoft rot, off-odors
Mycotoxin producersAspergillus, FusariumAflatoxin, ochratoxin, fumonisin
Important: Cooking/boiling may not destroy mycotoxins. Control moisture, storage and sort out visibly moldy/infested material.

11) Last-Minute Q & A
